The Year of Three Emperors - Dreikaiserjahr in Prussia, 1888

In 1888, the young German Empire faced a pivotal moment in its history. Wilhelm I (1797-1888), the first German Emperor and King of Prussia, passed away in Berlin on March 9th and was succeeded by his only son, Friedrich III (1831-1888). However, after a tragic reign of only 99 days, Friedrich III succumbed to throat cancer in Potsdam on June 15th and his eldest son then ascended the throne as Wilhelm II (1859-1941), marking the third reign within a single year. This abrupt transition was more than a change of figureheads; it was the intersection of the Empire’s pragmatic foundations and the burgeoning idealistic vanities that would eventually overstretch them. While a shared ideal is the necessary condition for a nation’s ascent, the tangible realization of its benefits remains the sufficient condition for its endurance—a balance that 1888 captures at its breaking point. Numismatically, the Kingdom of Prussia issued five distinct types of gold coins across two denominations in 1888, including two 10-Mark (Wilhelm I & Friedrich III) and three 20-Mark (Wilhelm I, Friedrich III, & Wilhelm II) specimens. These were minted in the standard "Goldmark" currency (1873-1914, ℳ︁) at the Berlin mint (1280 to date, A). This specialized 1888 collection represents a combined total of 28.673 g of fine gold, serving as a physical archive of the Empire’s most turbulent year of succession.

When his older brother, Friedrich Wilhelm IV, suffered a stroke in 1857, Wilhelm Friedrich Ludwig von Preußen—then still a prince—stepped from the shadows to assume the regency.[1] At the time, few would have envisioned that this quintessential Prussian officer, traditional and unassuming, would become the vanguard of military reform and the architect of German unification.

In 1861, the man once derided as "Kartätschenprinz" was formally crowned King Wilhelm I of Prussia. Although he remained at ideological odds with the parliament, he maintained a stance of prudent neutrality, seeking institutional ways to resolve political deadlock.[2] In the second year of his reign, Wilhelm I made a decision that would redirect the trajectory of European history: he recalled Otto von Bismarck from his embassy in France to serve as Minister-President.

Under the tactical brilliance of Helmuth von Moltke and the organizational rigor of War Minister Albrecht von Roon, Wilhelm I and his "Eiserner Kanzler" unified the German states—excluding Austria—through three wars spanning seven years.[3] Following the collapse of the Second French Empire, the King of Prussia was proclaimed Deutscher Kaiser in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles on January 18, 1871. This act established an empire of 25 states, ushering in an era that laid the socio-economic and institutional bedrock for all subsequent developments in German history.[4]

Ten months after the Empire’s founding, Wilhelm I enacted the Gesetz, betreffend die Ausprägung von Reichsgoldmünzen, Imperial Gold Coinage Act, introducing the "Goldmark" to the world stage.[5] These coins were initially struck in denominations of 10 and 20 Mark, consisting of a 900 Au/100 Cu alloy. By statutory definition, each German pound (Pfund, 500 g) of fine gold was to yield exactly 1,395 Mark.[6] Consequently, the 10-Mark coin contained 3.584 g of fine gold (3.982 g gross), while the 20-Mark coin contained 7.168 g (7.965 g gross, Fig. 1)—specifications that would remain the Empire's fiscal anchor for nearly half a century, although the Münzgesetz of 1873, Coinage Act, mandated further standardizations for the reverse motifs, the denomination inscription, and the positioning of the mintage year (Fig. 2 & 3).[7]

Unlike the gradual transitions seen elsewhere, the German Empire’s adoption of the gold standard was remarkably radical. Fueled by the 5 billion francs in French war reparations, the emerging superpower possessed the physical bullion to force a monetary revolution.[8] The Münzgesetz of 1873 pushed this further, attempting to replace silver with 5-Mark gold coins. This aggressive move away from bimetallism toward a monometallic gold standard triggered a global "gold scramble,"[9] contracting the global money supply and deepening the "Long Depression" that followed the 1873 financial crisis.[10] Politically, this turmoil prompted the Empire’s decisive shift from free trade to protectionism—the "Alliance of Steel and Rye."[11]

This radical policy, however, clashed with practical reality. The 5-Mark gold coins (containing 1.991 g of gold, Fig. 4) were a nuisance in daily life: they were too small for secure handling and suffered from excessive wear.[12] Their production was abandoned after only two years. Yet, due to the population-based silver limits stipulated in the 1873 Act and the lingering circulation of old Vereinsthalers,[13] the minting of 5-Mark silver coins did not immediately resume. It was only with the accession of Friedrich III in 1888 that the 5-Mark silver denomination truly returned to the regular minting schedule.

In January 1888, as new dies were fitted and molten gold flowed to strike the year's first batch of coinage bearing Wilhelm I’s profile, no one could have foreseen the impending twilight. Just over two months later, the 90-year-old Emperor would suddenly pass away at Schloss Charlottenburg due to a mere catarrhal infection,[14] leaving the young, vast empire to navigate a turbulent and unpredictable new century on its own.

When Wilhelm I passed away, the young Empire mourned the loss of its venerable founder. Yet, among the progressives, there was a quiet, fervent hope; Crown Prince Friedrich Wilhelm and his consort, Victoria, were widely seen as the vanguard of liberal reform.[15] The friction between the Crown Prince and the "Eiserner Kanzler" Bismarck had been a long-standing shadow over the court, spanning fundamental disagreements from the policy of "Blood and Iron" to the critical limitation of executive power in favor of parliamentary sovereignty.[16]

Though the Empire’s gold coinage saw no aesthetic upheaval during the brief reign (Fig. 5 & 6), the inscriptions bore an indelible political mark. By the time the 1888 gold coins were struck with the regnal name "Friedrich III," a profound debate over the Empire’s identity—whether it was to be a "German" or a "Prussian" entity—had been momentarily decided. Despite Bismarck’s heavy-handed pressure to adopt the style "Friedrich I" to signal a fresh, imperial beginning, the Emperor steadfastly insisted on the Prussian ordinal "III."[17] This was a rare, decisive political victory in his truncated reign: a declaration that the new Empire must remain anchored in the historic legal lineage of the House of Hohenzollern.[17]

However, on June 15, 1888, at the Neues Palais in Potsdam—the very cradle of Prussian zenith—throat cancer claimed the life of Friedrich III.[19] With his final breath, Germany’s most promising opportunity for a peaceful transition toward a constitutional monarchy vanished into the twilight.

Following the death of Frederick III in June 1888, Wilhelm II ascended the throne. He suffered from congenital Erb’s palsy, resulting in a withered left arm—a physical disability that arguably fostered a compensatory psychology and an obsessive need for prestige.[20] Consequently, after a brief period of inheriting his grandfather’s restrained foreign policy and the understated gold coin reverse (Fig. 7), he dismissed Bismarck in 1890. In doing so, he thoroughly rejected the "high-wire act" of Bismarckian diplomacy that had secured the Empire’s survival, driving Germany instead toward strategic isolation and a ruinous arms race.[21]

With the Empire’s "pilot" forced to step down, the new chancellor, Leo von Caprivi, was unable to curb the Kaiser’s erratic ambitions. Through inflammatory public statements and volatile maneuvers, the strategic stability provided by the Reinsurance Treaty fractured, leaving the German Empire increasingly encircled.[22] Simultaneously, his Weltpolitik plunged the nation into the quagmire of colonial friction.[23] These ambitions were mirrored in a redesign of the national symbols: on the reverse of the gold coinage, the eagle became more robust and majestic, its wings spreading to occupy nearly the entire surface, while the Prussian shield was reduced to a nearly unrecognizable scale (Fig. 8). He was no longer content to be a European monarch; he sought to lead a global empire.

It is worth noting that while these hasty policies led to political isolation, they did stimulate industrial and naval growth, spurred by scientific innovation under military pressure.[24] However, this militarism reached its symbolic peak in 1913. That year, Wilhelm II radicalized the standard circulating 20-mark gold coin by altering the obverse to a bust of himself in the uniform of the Gardes du Corps (Fig. 9). While military portraits were common on circulating commemorative coins across Europe, such a move breaks a century-old Prussian tradition of humble, non-military portraits on circulating currency.[25] At a time when the nation was embroiled in a hopeless arms race and the sparks of revolution were beginning to ignite across Europe, Wilhelm II remained obsessed with projecting his most heroic military image; his blind self-confidence in the face of the pre-war crisis seemed utterly reasonable.[26]

As the German forces under Hindenburg and Ludendorff failed to achieve a decisive victory in World War I, the Empire entered its twilight. The November Revolution of 1918 brought the Hohenzollern rule to a definitive end; Wilhelm II abdicated and fled to the Netherlands. Regrettably, even in exile, he harbored fantasies of restoration, even sending a congratulatory telegram to Adolf Hitler in 1940 in the desperate hope of a monarchist revival.[27] While some may still harbor a nostalgic sympathy for the House of Hohenzollern and the Empire's lost glory,[28] a leader who abandons reality for fantasy cannot lead a nation of "poets and warriors" away from danger; instead, he forces their wisdom and strength to degenerate into senseless fervor within the machinery of war.

The evolution of gold coins during the reign of Wilhelm II was not merely a shift in heraldry, but rather a case of “strategic losses stemming from overconfidence.” From the restraint of 1888 to the flamboyance of 1913, the wider the eagle’s wings spread across the coin’s surface, the more the empire’s strategic space was squeezed. When he donned that magnificent Guards’ uniform on the coin, he likely never realized that it was not the empire’s coronation, but rather a golden elegy he had personally composed for his ancestors’ legacy
