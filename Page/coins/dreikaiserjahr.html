The Year of Three Emperors - Dreikaiserjahr in Prussia, 1888

In 1888, King of Prussia and the first German Emperor, Wilhelm I (1797-1888), died in Berlin on 9th March and was succeeded by his only son Friedrich Wilhelm, as Friedrich III (1831-1888). After a reign of 99 days, Friedrich III died of throat cancer in Potsdam on 15th June and his eldest son, Friedrich Wilhelm, ascended the throne as Wilhelm II (1859-1941). To the best of our current knowledge, in 1888, the Kingdom of Prussia issued five types of coins in two denominations, including two 10-Mark gold coins (W1 & F3) and three 20-Mark gold coins in the currency of the "Goldmark" (1873-1914, ℳ︁), which were minted at the Berlin mint (1280 to date, A). The collection contains a total of 28.673 g fine gold.

When Wilhelm I passed away, the young Empire mourned the loss of its venerable founder. Yet, among the progressives, there was a quiet, fervent hope; Crown Prince Friedrich Wilhelm and his consort, Victoria, were widely seen as the vanguard of liberal reform.[1] The friction between the Crown Prince and the "Iron Chancellor" Bismarck had been a long-standing shadow over the court, spanning fundamental disagreements from the policy of "Blood and Iron" to the critical limitation of executive power in favor of parliamentary sovereignty.[2]

Though the Empire’s gold coinage saw no aesthetic upheaval during the brief reign (Fig. 5 & 6), the inscriptions bore an indelible political mark. By the time the 1888 gold coins were struck with the regnal name "Friedrich III," a profound debate over the Empire’s identity—whether it was to be a "German" or a "Prussian" entity—had been momentarily decided. Despite Bismarck’s heavy-handed pressure to adopt the style "Friedrich I" to signal a fresh, imperial beginning, the Emperor steadfastly insisted on the Prussian ordinal "III."[3] This was a rare, decisive political victory in his truncated reign: a declaration that the new Empire must remain anchored in the historic legal lineage of the House of Hohenzollern.[4]

However, on June 15, 1888, at the Neues Palais in Potsdam—the very cradle of Prussian zenith—throat cancer claimed the life of Friedrich III.[5] With his final breath, Germany’s most promising opportunity for a peaceful transition toward a constitutional monarchy vanished into the twilight.
